Gravitation in the vicinity of a black hole


Summary
I- General theory
II- Photon theory

 In a relativist frame, the movement of a particle of a mass M is observed in the gravitational field , without spherical symmetry nor rotation, placed at the origin of the spatial coordinates r, θ, φ.
The system is found in an empty space-time. The Schwarzschild metric is used as it is conveniently a solution to Einstein's equations. It describes the space-time geometry when it is distorted by a gravitational field of a spherical static (no rotation) mass with no charge and surrounded by void. This mass can describe a star, a planet or a black hole.
The Schwarzschild metric yields the following :

$$ ds^{2}=-e^{\lambda}dr^{2} - r^{2}(d\theta^{2} + sin^{2}\theta d\phi^{2}) + e^{\lambda} c^{2} dt^{2}$$


It is shown that :

$$ \lambda + \nu = 0 ~~~~e^{\nu} = 1 -\frac{2m}{r}$$

 r is the distance between the centers of the particle and of the solid body while c is the speed of light ( c = 299792458 m.s-1 ).
It is shown that m, the radius of the body of mass M, is :

$$ m=\frac{GM}{c^{2}}$$

G is the gravitational constant and is 6,6742.10-11 m3kg-1s-2.

The examined possible paths of a free particle are those for which :

$$\theta = \frac{1}{2}\pi$$

The "straight lines" in our space curved by the mass M are called geodesics.
Two of the geodesics equations are:

$$ \frac{d^{2}t}{ds^{2}} + \frac{d\nu}{dr} \frac{dr}{ds} \frac{dt}{ds} ~~~~~\frac{d^{2}\phi}{ds^{2}} + \frac{2}{r} \frac{dr}{ds} \frac{d\phi}{ds} + \frac{2}{tg\theta}\frac{d{\theta}}{ds} \frac{d\phi}{ds}=0$$

 τ is the proper time of the particle. It's the time measured in the system of coordinates of the particle, it's the system where the latter is stationary.
The primary integrals are inferred of the two previous equations :

$$ \left( 1-\frac{2m}{r} \right) \frac{dt}{d\tau} =E ~~~~~r^{2}\frac{d\phi}{d\tau}=cL$$

The expression of L can be determined with the equation of the tangential component of the particle speed as a function of is own proper time τ :

$$ v_{\phi}(\tau) = r(\tau)\frac{d\phi}{d\tau}$$

The initial conditions lead to the following:

$$ L=\frac{v_{\phi}(\tau_{0})r(\tau_{0})}{c}$$

Back to the equation of the Schwarzschild metric. It can be written as :

$$ \left( \frac{dr}{d\tau} \right)^{2} + c^{2}\left( 1-\frac{2m}{r}\right) \left( 1+\frac{L^{2}}{r^{2}} \right) = c^{2} E^{2}$$

The equation of E can be written based upon the equation of the radial component of the particle speed as a function of is own proper time τ :

$$ v_{r}(\tau) = \frac{dr}{d\tau}$$

With the initial conditions, the following is obtained:

$$ E=\sqrt{\frac{v_{r}(\tau_{0})^{2}}{c^{2}} + \left( 1-\frac{2m}{r(\tau_{0})} \right) \left( 1+ \frac{L^{2}}{r^{2}(\tau_{0})}\right)}$$

 Deriving the new metric equation and for non circular path, the differential equation used to simulate a particle around a solid body is deduced to be:

$$ \frac{d^{2}r}{d\tau^{2}}=-\frac{1}{2} \frac{dV}{dr}$$

with the function V which is the effective potential given by :

$$ V(r)=c^{2}\left( 1-\frac{2m}{r} \right) \left( 1+\frac{L^{2}}{r^{2}}\right)$$


 RS is named the 'Horizon of the black hole' or 'Schwarzschild radius'. It represents the spherical area which is the limit from where light and matter cannot escape.
 For better illustration, we can represent an observer near to the horizon and an observer far away from the black hole. Time flows differently for those two observers. Near to the horizon the observer has the feeling that time flows more quickly for his colleague,who stays further away from the black hole than the former. Conversely, the observer staying far from the black hole sees his colleague's motions slackening.
 The time dilatation can be observed when the remote observer sees an object fall into the black hole. The possible signals emmited by the object will be more and more spaced out, giving the impression to the distant oberserver that the object has stopped just before the horizon.
 Let dt be a time interval for the observer and dτ for the particle.
The time dilatation formula between the particle and the observer is given by:

$$ d\tau=\frac{1}{E}\left( 1-\frac{2m}{r(\tau)}\right)dt$$

 As one approaches a black hole, the tidal effects gain more importance.

This presentation, as in the previous versions, relies on the lessons of cosmology made by J-P Cordoni.

Photon trajectory

The Schwarzschild metric ds is the product of the speed of light with proper time dτ. The latter is theorecticaly measured by a clock following the world line of the particle in question. The world line of a photon is indisputably impossible to follow. Therefore dτ = ds = 0.

The primary integrals of the two geodesic equations mentioned earlier remain consistent if the proper time is replaced by a parameter a. We keep θ = π/2 (dθ = 0). With all that is mentioned above, the Schwarzschild metric takes the following form:

$$ \left( \frac{dr}{da} \right)^{2} + V(r) = c^{2}E^{2}$$

$$ \frac{V(r)}{c^{2}}=\frac{L^{2}}{r^{2}} \left( 1-\frac{2m}{r}\right)$$

The derivative of (1) yields:

$$ \frac{d^{2}r}{da^{2}}=-\frac{1}{2} \frac{dV}{dr} (2)$$

In the simulation part the user is asked to enter a speed vφ(0). This enables the calculation of L and vr(0).

$$ L=\frac{1}{cr}v_{\phi(0)}~~~~~~~v_{r}(0)^{2}=c^{2}-v_{\phi}(0)^{2}$$

vφ(0) imposes a value on à vr(0), therefore E can be calculated with (1). The photon's path is graphed by applying Runge-Kutta's method on (2).
